Accountablity in Chicago Public Schools

Overview

The Question

In modern education, standardized tests predominate discussions of educational quality and accountability. Students, teachers, and schools are measured by these seemingly impartial markers of student achievement. But is it reasonable to use the same metric to evaluate all schools, even if the composition of their student populations is not uniform? Are there correlations between identifiable subgroups of students that point to trends in notions of successful schools? In this analysis we examine the relationship between accountability status (via School Quality Rating Policy (SQRP) scores) and subgroup markers including percentage of students classified for speical education services through, the creation of an Individualized Education Plan (IEP) or a 504, percentage of students who qualify for Free and Reduced Lunch (FRL), and percentage of students who are identified and bilingual who may be provided support with English for Speakers of Other Languages (ESOL). We examined these factors in elementary and high schools.

Background on SQRP Scores

SQRP scores are the metric Chicago Public Schools (CPS) utilizes to compare the quality of schools within the district. The stated intent of these scores is to communicate to the community the academic success of particular schools, identify and recognize high achieving and high growth schools to determine best practices, provide a framework for goal setting, identify schools for support, and guide Board decisions. CPS SQRP scores are obtained from data collected the previous year. Thus, the scores accessed for this analysis were collected during the 2016-2017 school year. The determination of SQRP scores involves many factors which are given different weights. For example, the calculation for elementary schools takes into account student growth on the NWEA (Northwest Evaluation Association) assessment, student attendance, growth of priority groups, percentage of students making national average growth, among others. Background on Demographic Factors

Our focus in this analysis is how these SQRP scores assigned to each school are related to student demographic markers. Specifically, we examined percent of students classified as special education, percent of students identified as bilingual, and percent of students eligible for free and reduced lunch.Do any of these markers show a correlation with the SQRP scores schools receive from the district? If so, what is the nature of that correlation and what can that tell us about other how other factors impact the level of the SQRP score? Because these scores have the potential to have a dramatic impact on enrollment in specific schools and public perception of the quality of neighborhood schools, the stakes are very high for faculty and staff.

Students can qualify for additional services under special education policies through creation of an Individualized Education Plan (IEP) or utilization of a 504 which refers to Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973. Section 504 requires schools "provide free and appropriate public education for students." These classifications comprise a wide array of needs and designations including, but not limited to, "physically impaired" (PI), "cognitively impaired" (CI), "emotionally impaired" (EI), or specific learning disability (SLD). Within CPS, between 13-14 percent of students have an IEP and 3-4 percent of students have a 504 on file. CPS recognizes the achievement gap between students with IEP and those without and has instituted programs to attempt to diminish that gap.

Bilingual classifications are also varied. Some programs consist of a self-contained component of direct English language instruction. Other programs are more immersive with significant proportions of the school day devoted to instruction in the language spoken in the students' home. Once students are identified for English language support through self-reporting or testing, they remain in the program until it is determined they no longer require support, or their parents or guardians remove them from the program.

Free and reduced lunch designation is commonly used to identify low-income students. While schools mostly use this demographic indicator to determine which students receive nutritional support, researchers will use these statistics to make assumptions about resources available to those students in their homes compared to those who do not qualify. The Federal government sets the parameters for FRL. For the 2017-2018 school year, the "guidelines for free meals and milk and reduced price meals were obtained by multiplying the year 2017 Federal income poverty guidelines by 1.30 and 1.85, respectively, and by rounding the result upward to the next whole dollar." As of fall 2014, CPS has provided all students, regardless of their qualification for FRL, with one free breakfast and one free lunch per day.

Elementary Schools

Schools designated as Elementary Schools are those with students in pre-K through 8th grade. There are 473 schools that make up this subgroup within Chicago Public Schools. Parents in CPS have many options regarding where they send their students including Neighborhood, Charter, Classical, Contract, Magnet, Gifted, and Small. Because of all of theses parental choices, the rating systems for schools is a key factor in the decision-making process for parents. A low score could lead to reduced enrollment and repercussions for the faculty and staff. That is why it is essential for these scores to be accurate depictions of the quality of the school and not merely a reshuffling of test scores. The demographics of these schools need to be taken into account where these demographics could impact the SQRP score.

When comparing the SQRP scores to the demographic data indicators special education percentage, bilingual percentage, and free and reduced lunch percentage. With regard to special education, there appears to be statistically significant a negative correlation with SQRP scores. This implies that as the proportion of students with special education designations--primarily those with IEPs and 504s--increases, the SQRP scores decrease. This impact could imply that schools with greater percentages of special education designated students are being impacted disproportionately by this scoring system. A similar trend was clear with free and reduced lunch. As the percentage of those students increased, the SQRP score decreased. The bilingual designation appears to have the opposite impact signaling a direct relationship with SQRP scores.

High Schools

High Schools serve students in 9th through 12th grades. Within the Chicago Public School System, there are 116 high schools. Similar to elementary school parents, parents of high schoolers have many options for their child's education. Often high school parents perceive the stakes as higher for their students to be in the best school at this level. They are looking for schools to best prepare their students for the next phase of their lives. This makes the competition intense for schools thought to be best. Thus school publicized rating systems, like the SQRP, can have a massive impact on the schools evaluated.

An examination of the regression of demographic data and SQRP score assignment for high schools shows a similar pattern to that of elementary schools. Both special education and free and reduced lunch show statistically significant relationships. In both instances as the demographic indicator (special education and free and reduced lunch) increases, the SQRP score decreases. High school bilingual students also show a similar trend to that of elementary schools, as the percentage of bilingual students increases so does the SQRP score.

Conclusion

By examining the data provided by CPS, we found that schools with higher percentages of students designated as special education or higher percentages of students who qualify for free or reduced lunch have a significantly lower SQRP scores. Schools with higher percentages of students identified as bilingual have the opposite relationship showing a correlation with higher SQRP scores. While these results are compelling, it is important to recognize that much like the SQRP scores, these relationships do not tell the entire story. An issue as complicated as school quality can not be distilled down to only a few components. And while these components all surely play a role, the interaction between them is not easily definable and likely differs from school to school.

The differing effects of the demographic data may be related to school attendance rates among these groups. As the percentages of students designated special education or qualifying for FRL increases, the attendance percentage at these schools decreases. The opposite is true for schools with higher percentages of bilingual students. As the percentage of students designated bilingual increases, attendance does as well. Because attendance is a significant component of the SQRP calculation, this relationship is directly proportional. For elementary schools, it comprises 20 percent of the score, and for high schools, it comprises 10 percent of the score. While it is important to recognize that these correlations do not imply any causation, there is a logical component to the notion that higher attendance rates leads to increased test scores and by extension better ratings in the SQRP.

While variables such as attendance and test scores are the primary factors utilized to compare the quality of schools within CPS, this study suggests that SQRP scores may not be a completely reliable metric for measuring school quality. Without considering the effect of demographics on scores, important intervening factors are disregarded which could paint an incorrect picture of a school to the community. Presently, SQRP scores take into account markers of student achievement that do not entirely reflect school environment. In order to ensure a more equitable rating system, the components should include factors that attempt to suitably address the demographic characteristics of the school.
